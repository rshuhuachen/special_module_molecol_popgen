4  Week 8 - gene flow

4.1 Overview

In today’s practical, we will use a published microsatellite dataset on Antarctic fur seals to investigate gene flow and population differentiation.

The Antarctic fur seal (Arctocephalus gazella) is polygynous, highly site faithful, and has a generation time of around ten years. Despite breeding on all of the major sub-Antarctic islands, it was driven to the brink of extinction by the 18th and 19th century sealing industry. Harvesting begun shortly after South Georgia was discovered by Captain James Cook in 1775 and reached its peak at South Georgia in 1800 when over 100,000 seals were harvested. Shortly afterwards, with the discovery of the other sub-Antarctic islands, a wave of sealing spread across the entire sub-Antarctic region, culminating in an estimated 1.7 million Antarctic fur seals being taken.

By the turn of the 20th century, Antarctic fur seals were considered all but extinct and the species was placed under legal protection. For the best part of three decades and despite several dedicated sub-Antarctic expeditions, very few individuals were sighted at the traditional breeding grounds with the exception of Bouvetøya where a small population of an uncertain fur seal species was observed in the late 1920s. However, the Discovery expedition of 1936 observed a small breeding population of Antarctic fur seals with 12 pups at South Georgia, which grew rapidly in the 1960s and 1970s to reach an estimated five million individuals by 1999. As breeding populations were not observed at the other sub-Antarctic islands until the 1950s to the 1980s, several authors have speculated that the former geographic distribution of this species was likely recolonized from South Georgia. However, a number of studies have reported genetic differences among some of these populations, pointing towards a more complex picture in which more than one relict population may have survived sealing.

The Antarctic fur seal therefore provides with an interesting dataset to explore population structure which is the consequence of its sealing history.

4.5 Exercise 4

Next, we want to visualise the genetic structure using a principal component analysis (PCA). Microsatellite datasets often contain many loci and many alleles, making it difficult to directly visualize genetic differences among individuals or populations. A PCA is a statistical method that simplifies this complex genetic information by reducing it to a few major axes of variation.

PCA works by transforming the genetic data (allele frequencies or genotypes) into new variables called principal components. Each principal component represents a direction in the data that captures as much genetic variation as possible. The first principal component explains the largest amount of variation, the second explains the next largest amount, and so on.

When individuals are plotted along these components, genetically similar individuals cluster together, while genetically different individuals appear farther apart. In population genetics, this allows us to visualize population structure, identify genetically distinct groups, and detect individuals with mixed ancestry or possible migrants.

In this practical, we will use PCA to explore whether individuals cluster according to their sampling populations so we can better understand among which populations there is gene flow. Read chapter 6.2 of the adegenet vignette on principal component analyses and build a plot where each colour indicates a population. Does the PCA correspond to the pairwise Fst values we just calculated? And based on the PCA plot, where do you think migration/gene flow occurs?

Tips

First, replace the NA values with the mean using scaleGen. Next, compute a PCA using the dudi.pca function To plot the results, you want to colour them according to the populations of seal. We can plot using the s.class function. Plot the first two PC’s on the x- and y-axis respectively, and you can add additional aesthetic options using e.g. cpoint and grid, see ?s.class.

Code and answer
Code 
# first we have to replace NA's with the mean
X <- scaleGen(seal, NA.method="mean")

# calculate the PCA
pca <- dudi.pca(X,cent=FALSE,scale=FALSE,scannf=FALSE,nf=3) 

# generate 8 random colours to label our plot
col <-funky(8) 

# plot the first two PC's (PC1 on the x axis, PC2 on the y axis)
s.class(pca$li, pop(seal), xax=1, yax=2, col=transp(col,.6), axesell=FALSE, cstar=0, cpoint=2, grid=FALSE)
title("PCA Antarctic fur seal")
abline(v=0,h=0,col="grey",lty=2)

We previously identified that Fst values between population 2 and 7 are highest: this makes sense in the PCA because these two populations are spread apart. The x-axis shows PC1 which shows the most variation. Since population 2 and 7 are on opposite ends of the x-axis, this indeed makes sense. Population 6 and 7 are right next to each other / overlap in the PCA, consistent with them having the lowest pairwise Fst values.

We see four main clusters in the PCA: the first one contains population 5, 7 and 8, the second one population 1 and 2, the third one population 4 and 5 and lastly, population 3. It is likely that gene flow occurs within these four clusters.
